Written Description of Journey Components

Anchor & View

The map represents the pathway toward scheduling a mental health appointment. It relates to a "point-of-access" journey as opposed to the broader continuum of mental health care at large. It represents a micro view of the process from identifying a need for help and then working through the necessary steps to schedule an appointment. We chose to limit the scope of the map to this narrow timeframe because most of the interview data we collected centered around this timeframe. The structural and behavioral barriers that participants encountered while trying to access care were prevalent throughout the interviews; there was very little discussion regarding the continuum of ongoing treatment.

Start & End Points

The start point of the journey begins with the individual realizing that they are struggling with their mental health and that they need help. This eventually leads to the end point of setting an appointment with a professional. This is not a definitive destination and it is continuously evolving. Based on our interviews with the participants, we found that although all of them would get to a point of setting the appointment, there was still a need for further adjustment with who their long term provider would be.

Inflection Points & Milestones

The markers of progress are as follows:

1. They become concerned about their mental health.
   a. As the individual begins to experience overwhelm after handling their experience on their own for a long time. They realize they become concerned with their mental health.

2. Deciding to seek help.
   a. After realizing that they cannot handle it on their own, they go through a period of time where they deliberate on whether they can handle it or not. This eventually leads them to decide to seek help.

3. Researching and searching for professional support.
   a. They begin researching their options and searching for a provider. They may get recommendations from their friends or family, use the school counselors, or find someone online with services like ZocDoc. They're looking for someone who they can relate to through gender, culture, lifestyle, or their social norms.

4. Scheduling an appointment.
   a. After they believe they've found a provider who they're comfortable with, they begin the process of scheduling an appointment. This can take time due to schedules, insurance, cost, and location. Once they've scheduled an appointment, then begins another step in the journey of finding the best fit.

Stages

1. Between realization of a problem to deciding to seek help.
2. From deciding to seek help to finding a professional to work with.
3. From finding a professional to work with to scheduling an appointment.

After reviewing our data, we used tags to denote the timeline, such as "became aware, made a decision, intent, and initiation. We managed these tags using web applications like Atlas.ti and EnjoyHQ. Additionally, we discussed what themes we saw across interviews.

Enablers & Barriers

The strongest and most consistent barrier to access was navigating the healthcare system ‚Äì insurance complexity, provider availability, scheduling friction (Hypothesis 3). Insurance barriers were more frequently identified than cost barriers, with one participant (KC004), having reached his session limit before he could be clinically diagnosed, being denied additional sessions by his therapist who believed that the participant did not meet the requirements for treatment. Structural barriers also included geographic location; all rural participants and those from rural backgrounds reported a lack of providers in their town(s) and that telehealth services were not available to them because of poor Internet connectivity.

Psych-behavioral barriers can best be characterized as a threshold issue; participants consistently undermined their own suffering. Stigma was rarely identified as a direct barrier to participation; the majority of participants identified stigma as a phenomenon that was perceived in either other people or previous communities from which they had moved away from. Social and identity-based enablers were found to be nearly exclusively present; peer normalization, high status peers' willingness to seek help and, notably, one participant's motivation for seeking help was based upon the fact that she had always been a mental health advocate and would have appeared to be a hypocrite if she did not act on her own values (KC004).

Process

The thematic map was generated from the data of ten interviews with usable response data (RM001, EF002, FR003, KC004, ND005, AR006, SP007, EP008, DC010). Five emerging themes were confirmed across the full interview set: crisis threshold, peer influence as tipping point, navigation maze, generational divide, and trust requires authenticity. Hypothesis #1 (Information Overload), was NOT supported by the interviewee's responses. Hypothesis #3 (Navigation/Insurance as Barrier), was strongly supported by the interviewee's responses. The interviewee, KC004, provided additional context to the insurance cap, rural barriers to access, and how previous advocacy efforts provided an additional source of motivation to seek help. There was one non-help-seeking participant in this study who was a no-show, the interviewee EF009, therefore there was no response data to synthesize.

Goals

Getting help with the overwhelm

The goal is not to create a new version of yourself, but to be relieved. All of these participants were concerned with ending their anxiety about draining themselves in every way - emotionally, physically, financially etc. to continue to go through their daily routine. Participant RM001 wanted to stop simply doing things on autopilot, Participant FR003 wanted to receive some assistance with processing the postpartum physical/emotional changes that they did not realize were happening, and Participant EP008 wanted to have someone assist them in "fixing herself". The longer-term goal underneath all of that was restored functioning ‚Äî showing up at work, in relationships, and in daily life without it taking everything they had.

Restore functioning Find trusted support Regain control
Motivation

A breaking point, not a plan

Most people do not take the initiative to get help because they cannot find motivation to make that commitment to action. That motivation usually comes from a moment of unrelenting pain (failing school, feeling emotionally numb while traveling, crying in your own driveway) when the cost of getting help is less than the cost of staying away from help due to the overwhelming fear of seeking help.

Seeing peers get professional help can make it seem more real and possible to seek such services as well.

"I sat in my driveway and got super emotional. I was just like - something is wrong." - EP008
Mental Models

If I'm still functioning, I don't need help.

High Self-Concept & Self-Efficacy

This archetype uses external performance as evidence that things are okay. RM001 described his thinking before seeking help as straightforward: if you're functioning, you don't need a therapist. EF002 assumed her anxiety was just common or normal, something everyone dealt with, and it only manifested at night when it was just her and her thoughts. FR003 was "working on autopilot" through postpartum depression and didn't register as significantly affecting daily life. The logic is almost self-sealing because they're still showing up, and the problem doesn't feel real enough to act on.

"‚Ä¶as long as I'm functioning and working and getting stuff done, I don't need a therapist." - RM001
Self-Image: My concerns aren't that bad

Most participants compared their perceived levels of pain to an increasingly high pain benchmark to minimize their own pain. ND005 referred to this directly, stating he was afraid that the pain he felt would not be "important" to a therapist enough to justify a session for someone else who was experiencing more severe issues; therefore, why would his concerns be worthy of the same amount of attention? SP007 discussed having grown up with a good education, good friends, and good parents; however, stated she believed she required a tangible reason (such as a bad situation) to attend therapy as opposed to simply attending due to her perceived need for help. This perception causes the "bar" for "bad enough" to continue to rise and subsequently move further away from each participant's current level of distress.

"I would definitely say like there have been times when my stress has been a little bit higher than normal‚Ä¶But I wouldn't say necessarily struggle with mental health, no." - DC010
Uncertainty: Will I even find someone I trust?

Before participants contacted therapists, many had also taken into account the perceived hurdles they would encounter. SP007 identified finding an adequate therapist as extremely difficult, citing the outdated provider lists, the inability of hospital phone systems to properly route calls, and the student therapists leaving after six month rotations as examples of these obstacles. EF002 noted that initially, there was no single resource to provide information on locating a therapist, and she did not have a full understanding of therapy when she began her search. As such, the participant's expectations encountering difficulty in their pursuit of a therapist were validated as benign true for most of them. Validation of those expectations increased the apparent size of the barrier for future attempts.

"It was hard to deep dive and understand everything about therapy. Hard to find therapists at the time. There wasn't anywhere you could go to find a list of therapists." ‚Äî EF002
Coping

Working the toolkit until it stops working

This archetype is using various coping mechanisms to handle their stress. These mechanisms included focusing on work and school, using humor, masking, and just acceptance for the way things were.

For example, RM001 described always moving to balance out his anxiety instead of seeking real help: staying busy, managing through motion rather than addressing the source. EF002 used hobbies, candles, friends, and pets to cope. ND005 used humor for years, masking as a very happy person, until they noticed it wasn't helping anymore and that the strategy they'd relied on had become unsustainable. FR003 was running on autopilot through postpartum depression without fully registering what was happening. EP008 handled many of their issues on their own for years, assuming everyone was struggling the same way, until they hit a wall as they entered college. The pattern across participants is consistent: they had strategies, and those strategies worked until they didn't. The decision to seek professional help comes at the end of that cycle, not the beginning. It's not avoidance; it's exhaustion of everything else first.

"I used humor to cope, but noticed it wasn't helping anymore." ‚Äî ND005
Norm Significance

Their social world sets the pace

What the people around them think, or what they imagine those people will think, shapes the timeline more than any individual decision. EF002 spent weeks running through imaginary conversations with her parents prior to talking to her mom, simply because she was worried about how her parents would react to this news. ND005 said he grew up within a cultural context of Indonesia that perceived asking for help as weakness (you were strong if you could do things on your own) and therefore carried this way of thinking into adulthood before realizing there was another way. RM001 stated that his "a-ha" moment came after seeing a friend go through the process and didn't want the same experience. It was not the internalization of the information, but the peer experience that actually caused his resistance to break down.

The same dynamic works in reverse too. KC004's social world was normalized when it came to mental health care. Therefore, the norms of her social world made removing the barriers to access to mental health care much easier than adding to them. Her advocacy background and social circle had helped create a culture that viewed seeking help as normal behavior, not deviant behavior. SP007 reported a similar culture existed among her peers; she reported that therapy was just something people did and they talked about it all the time. When her brother called her to tell her he had his first therapy session, she reacted positively. In addition to delaying the journey, norms can also complicate the journey. When the social environment is right, norms can speed along the journey.

"All my friends, like, we all know each other's prescription levels... when you're with your family, like, you know their negative habits... and when you see them go to therapy, you're happy that they're finally gonna be able to address those things." ‚Äî SP007
Written Description of Archetype Components

Goals & Motivation

The goals for this archetype is a return to normalcy and the avoidance of a terrible outcome. As their situation becomes more overwhelming, their realization that something isn't right and their concern for their mental health grows. This concern begins to drive them towards rectifying their situation. The motivation is intrinsic. It comes from the fear of continuing in a state of overwhelm and anxiety and the consequences of that.

Mental Models

Three mental models consistently presented with this archetype.

1. High Self-Concept / Self-Efficacy: If I'm functioning, I'm fine.
   a. These frameworks caused a delay in realization and action for the archetype. The belief that they're in control and do not need help, delayed their realization of becoming overwhelmed and seeking help.
   b. To shift this behavior, this archetype needed to see examples of people in their reference network who were going through similar things.

2. Self-Image: My concerns are not that bad or as bad as others.
   a. The belief that their concerns were not that bad or as bad as others also delayed their decision to seek help. Despite recognizing their stress levels and overwhelm, this archetype believes it's not that bad.
   b. To shift this behavior, this archetype had to realize what they were experiencing wasn't something minor and inconsequential.

3. Uncertainty: Will I be able to get an appointment, let alone one with someone I trust.
   a. The experience of uncertainty did not cause a delay in deciding to seek help, but it did cause a delay in finding help and a feeling of anxiety and frustration in finding someone suitable to work with.
   b. To shift this behavior, it's necessary to have more accessible resources for connecting with and finding diverse professionals.

Additional Components: Coping & Norm Significance

We selected coping and norm significance as our two additional components.

1. Coping was used as a way to accept or deny the existence of a problem whether it was subconscious or not. Coping helped the participants justify their high level of self-concept and efficacy.

2. Norm Significance connected to what was considered normal within each participants reference and social network. For each participant, they stated their friends and family would be supportive. This normalcy and support of mental health care helped prevent stigma based barriers. It didn't impact their self-image in a way that would prevent them from seeking help.

Process

The archetype was built primarily from six participants: RM001, EF002, FR003, ND005, SP007, and AR006, with supporting context from EP008 and DC010. We coded across transcripts for shared behavioral patterns, first the tendency to delay despite awareness, the use of coping strategies before reaching out, and the role of social context in finally acting. From there, we identified the underlying beliefs driving those patterns. KC004 was treated as an alternate pathway because their pre-existing mental health advocacy background and shorter delay didn't fit the pattern of the other participants. EF009 had no usable response data as they were a no-show. We focused on beliefs and behaviors over demographics, because what made the archetype coherent wasn't age or job title it was the shared logic of waiting, managing, and eventually breaking through.

Overall Process Note

The journey map and the behavioral archetype are telling the same story from two different angles. The journey map shows the path and where it breaks down structurally. The archetype explains why the path takes as long as it does behaviorally. A young adult who is functioning on the outside, burning through coping strategies, shaped by norms that make asking for help feel like admitting failure, and finally reaching a moment where staying put costs more than reaching out, that's the through line across RM001, EF002, FR003, ND005, SP007, EP008, and AR006. The structural barriers that SP007 and EF002 encountered with insurance and scheduling make a hard thing harder at exactly the moment someone has finally built up enough resolve to act. The social environments described by ND005, EF002, and RM001 either shortened or extended the wait. Together, these two frameworks are trying to make the invisible delay visible, because that's where the real friction lives, and that's what any meaningful intervention would need to address.
